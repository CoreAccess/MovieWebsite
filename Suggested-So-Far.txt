1. Architectural Post-Execution Assessment
The Strengths
Normalization (Tier 1): Moving to junction tables for genres and keywords is non-negotiable for scale. It allows for complex filtering (e.g., "Find movies that are both Cyberpunk and Noir") that JSON blobs make computationally expensive in standard SQL.

Schema.org Alignment: This is your "secret weapon" for the Agent Economy. By structuring your internal DB to map directly to schema.org/Movie and schema.org/UserReview, your JSON-LD generation becomes a simple view layer. This ensures that when an AI agent searches for "best movies to watch with a 5-year-old," your site is the easiest for it to parse and recommend.

Entity First-Classing (Tier 3): Elevating TVSeason and MovieSeries to their own tables allows you to store metadata (like custom posters or specific awards) at the franchise level, which TMDB often struggles to do cleanly.

Potential Concerns & Risks
The "Seeding" Bottleneck: Your plan requires deleting the DB and re-seeding from TMDB. As your schema grows to 49+ tables, the complexity of your "Seeder" service increases exponentially. You need to ensure your Go backend handles Rate Limiting and Data Integrity (e.g., what happens if TMDB returns a genre that doesn't exist in your genres table yet?).

Search Performance: With this many JOINs (Movies -> Genres -> Keywords -> Cast -> Production Co), simple SELECT queries will slow down.

Solution: Since you are moving to Google Cloud (PostgreSQL), you should plan to use Materialized Views for high-traffic pages (like a Movie's main profile) to pre-calculate these relationships.

2. Competitive Innovation: How to Beat the Giants
To be a "Town Hall" rather than just a library, the data needs to reflect human experience, not just technical specs.

Feature 1: The "Vibe" Vector (Beyond Mood Tags)
TMDB uses keywords; you should use Comparative Relationships.

The Innovation: A media_comparisons table.

The Data Hook: Let users vote on "If you liked Movie A, you will like Movie B because of [Relationship_Type]."

Why: This creates a much more powerful recommendation engine for your future vector database/ML training than simple keyword matching.

Feature 2: The "Social Graph" of Credits
IMDB shows you who worked on a film. You should show the Collaboration History.

The Innovation: A "Frequent Collaborators" view.

The Data Hook: Since you have media_crew and movie_cast normalized, you can query for "Directors who have worked with this Actor 3+ times."

Why: This is a "power-user" feature that cinema buffs love but currently have to search for manually.

Feature 3: The "Contextual" Timeline
The Innovation: Deep-linking on_this_day_events with posts.

The Data Hook: When it is the 10th anniversary of The Avengers, the "Town Hall" (your posts table) should automatically surface old "Staff Pick" reviews and historic discussions.

Why: It creates a sense of "living history" that keeps users coming back daily, not just when they watch something new.

3. Security and Scalability Deep-Dive
As you move to PostgreSQL on Google Cloud, keep these "Day 2" problems in mind:

Database Security
The edit_history Table: This is excellent for transparency, but as a "Town Hall," you will attract "data vandals."

Missing: You need a Reputation Requirement column in edit_history or a trusted_editor flag in the users table. Only users with a reputation_score (which you already have in users ) above X should have their edits go live instantly.


PII Leakage: You are storing google_id and facebook_id. Ensure these are never included in your API responses or your MCP server output.

Missing Architectural Elements
Vector Store Integration: You mentioned tokenizing the data.

Advice: Don't wait until the end. Add a description_vector (type vector using the pgvector extension) directly to your movies and tv_series tables now. This allows you to do "Semantic Search" (searching for "that movie about a sad robot in space") without needing a separate database.

Versioning for API/MCP: As you build for agents, your schema will change.

Missing: A schema_version or api_compatibility table to ensure that external agents don't break when you add Tier 5 or Tier 6 features.


Media Proxy/Cache: Your current DB stores TMDB image URLs.

Risk: If TMDB changes their URL structure or bans your referrer, your site goes dark.

Innovation: Use Google Cloud Storage to cache these images and store your internal URL in the media_images table.
